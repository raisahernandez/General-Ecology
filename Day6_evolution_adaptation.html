Evolution and adaptation

BIOL 350, California State University-Long Beach, Fall 2019

Environments play a major role in evolution. They affect the survival and reproduction of organisms, ultimately shaping phenotypes within populations. The effect of environments can be fast or slow, constant or acute but the many limitations we have in data collection (time, logistics, funding) often impede us to quantify such effects on evolution. On the other hand, there are climatic events that are so extreme that their effects on populations are immediate, opening opportunities to the quantification of selection. Donihue and colleagues (2018) measured morphology and performance in anole lizards before and after hurricanes Irma and Maria. Their main goal was to asses hurricane-induced selection.

Part II: Modeling the data with regressions


In Class 03 you learned about correlation analysis which estimates the correlation coefficient r. A more advanced but related statistics is the simple linear regression analysis. Simple linear regression is useful for finding linear relationships between two continuous variables. The main objective is to obtain a line that best fits the data. Check the figure below. The best fit line is the one for which total prediction error (data points in red) are as small as possible. Here, error is defined as the distance (green) between the point to the regression line (blue).

Figure 1. In linear regression, the observations (red) are assumed to be the result of random deviations (green) from an underlying relationship (blue) between a dependent variable (y) and an independent variable (x) (https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=4099808)



Therefore, linear regression - in contrast to correlation - gives a slope defining the change in the dependent variable by changing the independent variable by unit distance. The intercept in a linear regression gives the value of the dependent variable if the independent variable is set to zero. Finally, linear regression can give you a prediction.

Step 1. Fit a linear regression to the data from hypothesis 2 (below the continued example)

lm() is used to fit linear models in R. Just like for t-test, the first argument is the dependent variable, the second argument is the independent variable, both separated by ~.

# Linear regression between SVL and Femur for anoles from both origins
my_lm <- lm(anol$Femur~anol$SVL)
summary(my_lm)

Interpreting (some of the) R output

Call: The first item shown in the output is the formula R used to fit the data (the formula you just wrote).

Residuals: The next item in the model output are the residuals. Residuals are essentially the difference between the actual observed values from the dependent variable (femur length) and the values that the model predicted (the line). The Residuals section breaks it down into 5 summary points or descriptive statistics (minimum, first quantile, median, third quantile, maximum). These stats give you an idea of how symmetric (normally distributed) is your data.

Coefficients: Remember a linear equation: y = mx + b. The coefficient Estimate contains two rows; the first one is the intercept of the line (b). The intercept, in our example, is theoretically the expected length of a femur (-1.72 mm) when SVL = 0 mm. The second row in the Coefficients is the slope, or in our example, the effect SVL (independent/explanatory variable) has in femur length (dependent/response variable). The slope term in our model is saying that for every 1 mm increase in SVL, the femur length goes up by 0.26 mm (there is a positive relationship between these two variables).

Pr(>|t|) represents the p-value which in our example is < 0.05 (significant).

Multiple R-squared, Adjusted R-squared: The R-squared statistic provides a measure of how well the linear model is fitting the actual data. It always lies between 0 and 1 (like the coefficient of correlation, a number near 0 represents a regression that does not explain the variance in the response variable well and a number close to 1 does explain the observed variance in the dependent/response variable). In our example, the R-squared we get is 0.85. Or roughly 85% of the variance found in the response variable (femur length) can be explained by the predictor variable (SVL)! Thats a good fit!
